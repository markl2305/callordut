Commercial AV integration cost varies widely by room type, size, and system complexity — there's no single per-room number that holds across huddle rooms, conference rooms, and boardrooms. The variance comes from display size, audio coverage, video conferencing capability, and integration depth — not square footage alone. Because those variables compound quickly, the most reliable way to get an accurate number for a specific room is an itemized quote rather than a published range.

## What drives per-room AV cost

Three factors determine where a room lands in the pricing range.

**Display and audio scope** is the biggest variable. A 65-inch display with a soundbar and a single USB camera sits at the low end of the scale; a 100-inch LED wall with distributed ceiling speakers, a beamforming array mic, and a PTZ camera with auto-tracking sits at the high end. Display hardware alone spans a wide range depending on size and technology — this single line item can be the difference between a modest room and a significantly more expensive one.

**Video conferencing capability** adds cost in layers. A basic room with a single USB webcam and a room PC for Teams or Zoom is the entry tier. Mid-tier adds a dedicated video codec or conference appliance. High-end adds a dedicated video system (Crestron, Extron, Q-SYS) with auto-tracking, echo cancellation, and integration into the building's AV control system.

**Integration depth** separates the tiers. A standalone room with a local controller is the base. Integrated rooms connect to a central AV management system, have room scheduling displays, and share content with other spaces. Fully integrated rooms participate in a building-wide AV platform with centralized monitoring and control.

## Typical room types and relative cost tiers

### Huddle rooms (2–6 people)

Huddle rooms are the most common AV space in commercial buildings. Typical configuration: 55–65-inch display, soundbar or ceiling speakers, a USB video conferencing camera, and a room PC or built-in compute. The target is quick setup for ad-hoc collaboration.

Huddle rooms sit at the lowest cost tier in commercial AV — the smallest display size, the least audio complexity, and typically a USB-based conferencing setup rather than a dedicated codec. It's the fastest room type to spec and install.

### Conference rooms (8–16 people)

Conference rooms add audio coverage and display size. Typical configuration: 75–86-inch display or dual-display setup, distributed ceiling speakers, a beamforming or boundary mic array, and a dedicated video conferencing appliance or codec.

Conference rooms move up the tier on both audio coverage and display size. Cost climbs with the number of ceiling speakers, the sophistication of the mic array, and whether the room needs a dedicated video codec versus a simpler USB solution — plus whether it needs to integrate into a larger AV management system.

### Boardrooms (16–30+ people)

Boardrooms require the highest audio and video fidelity. Typical configuration: 98-inch+ display or LED wall, distributed audio with echo cancellation, a high-end PTZ or multi-camera video system, and a dedicated video conferencing or control platform.

Boardrooms sit at the top of the tier. Large-format displays or LED walls, full-coverage audio with echo cancellation, and high-end camera systems all add cost, and a boardroom with a custom LED wall and full building-wide integration is typically the most expensive room type in a facility.

### Training rooms and classrooms

Training rooms vary widely by size and purpose. A small training room with a projector or large display and a single audio zone runs in the conference room tier. Large training rooms with multiple audio zones, video distribution, and recording capability run higher.

Training room cost tracks room size and the number of audio zones — a single-zone room lands closer to a conference room, while a large multi-zone room with recording or live-streaming capability climbs toward boardroom-level complexity.

### Presentation and event spaces

Presentation spaces add complexity for lighting control, stage audio, and audience coverage. The cost is driven by the scale of the space and the production capability required.

Presentation spaces are the least predictable category because scope varies the most — lighting control, stage audio, and audience coverage all scale with the size and production value of the space, giving this room type the widest possible range of any on this list.

## The hidden cost: integration and commissioning

Hardware is only part of the cost. Integration and commissioning typically runs 30–50% of hardware cost for a properly engineered installation. This covers system programming, network configuration, audio tuning, and user training. Skipped here and the room won't perform as designed — the most common cause of underperforming AV installations.

## What a commercial AV estimate should include

A complete AV estimate breaks out hardware, installation labor, integration/commissioning, and training. If an estimate only shows a total without these line items, ask for the breakdown — you need to know what you're paying for and where the value sits.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the typical cost for a commercial conference room AV system?

There's no fixed number — conference room cost depends primarily on display technology, audio coverage, and whether the room integrates into a larger AV management system. It sits above huddle rooms and below boardrooms on the overall spectrum. The most accurate answer for a specific room comes from a site survey and an itemized quote.

### How much does AV integration cost per room for a huddle space?

Huddle rooms sit at the lowest cost tier in commercial AV, driven by their smaller display size, simpler audio setup, and typically USB-based (rather than dedicated codec) video conferencing. Exact cost still depends on the specific hardware selected — a quote is the only way to get a firm number.

### What drives the difference between a lower-cost and a higher-cost AV room?

The difference comes down to display size and technology, audio coverage quality, video conferencing capability, and integration depth. A lower-cost room typically has a smaller display, basic audio, and a USB video solution. A higher-cost room typically has an LED wall or large-format display, distributed audio with echo cancellation, a high-end video system, and integration into a central AV platform.

### Should I budget for integration and commissioning on top of hardware?

Yes. Integration and commissioning typically runs 30–50% of hardware cost for a properly engineered installation. This covers system programming, network configuration, audio tuning, and user training. Skipping this step is the most common cause of underperforming AV installations.

### Does per-room AV pricing include the video conferencing license?

Not always. Many AV estimates include the hardware but not the video conferencing license (Teams Rooms, Zoom Rooms, etc.). Ask your integrator whether the estimate includes the software license or if it's a separate cost.
